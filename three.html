Principle #3:

Deliver Working Software Frequently

The third Agile principle emphasizes frequent delivery of working software, ideally every few weeks to a couple of months.

This approach contrasts sharply with traditional "command-and-control" project management, which often struggles to accommodate changes without chaos. Agile tackles this by using iterative development with regular, short cycles (iterations). Each iteration concludes with a demo for the customer and a retrospective for the team, followed by planning for the next cycle.

This frequent delivery, coupled with constant checkpoints, enables early detection and integration of changes in a blame-free environment.

For traditional project managers, this provides a way to control deadlines and manage late-stage changes more effectively, as the entire team becomes involved in spotting issues earlier.

The project manager's role shifts from dictating tasks to collaborating with the team, ensuring everyone is aligned with overarching goals, which is facilitated by the clear, short-term objectives of each iteration and the collective responsibility for delivering working software.
